## 1. What this document is, and why it earns the adoption

This is the first Craig document written as an engineering artifact rather than a strategy memo, and it is the best-disciplined of the stream. The tier law is not a principle here, it is code: every KPI, widget, detector and recommendation carries a [T0] or [T1] tag, a [T1] element must name its [T0] fallback, and a Tier 1 output reaches a payload only when four independent conditions hold at once (tenant flag on, model enabled, regulatory status cleared, caller capability), enforced server side in the serializer with the fields omitted, not nulled, and tested in CI. The architecture earns equal praise: a one-way flow into a separate crtw schema where the ingestion layer is the only writer and nothing writes back to the transactional core, so CRTW structurally cannot mutate a case; a pseudonymized worker dimension with the identity map isolated behind an ACL that employer and leadership surfaces never join; and the k-anonymity guard applied at the point of detection, not just display, so a suppressed cohort never even generates an insight. The estimation methodology is the most honest treatment of projected money this venture has produced: ranges with named sources in preference order (published evidence, tenant observed effect, explicitly labeled Inferred), compounded estimates that widen rather than hide, a worked example, a standing section titled what would make this estimate wrong, and a closed loop that only promotes realized effects to a basis after the same sample-size guard as everything else. And the small touches show a specifier who has shipped: empty and low-data states are specified per screen, a detector run that writes nothing still logs so an empty result and a broken job are distinguishable, and Tier 1 models run dark from day one so clearance meets a validation history instead of a cold start.

## 2. Three flags, each with its repair

**First, the stack presupposition.** The document builds on NestJS, PostgreSQL schemas, logical decoding CDC, a React Native worker app and a React/Next.js dashboard app. That is the v2 wiring spec's stack, and Decision 1 from the platform blueprint (NestJS and PostgreSQL versus the series-ratified Next.js, Supabase and Capacitor) remains formally unruled. This spec is now the second backend document to assume the NestJS answer, and its Part 6 DDL, ETL and jobs design deepen the cost of ruling late. The repair is not technical, it is procedural: rule D1 before any Part 6 schema work begins. If the ruling is NestJS and PostgreSQL, this spec builds as written; if it is Supabase, the crtw schema, RLS, materialized rollups and scheduled jobs all have Supabase equivalents and the spec translates rather than breaks, but someone must say which world we are in, and this review formally asks for that ruling.

**Second, supervisor-cohort analytics belong in the electronic monitoring policy.** The Supervisor Coaching Detector is designed with real care: operational metrics only, transparent and appealable, the supervisor sees their own evidence, coaching framing with disciplinary language forbidden. But performance analytics computed over a supervisor's span is workplace monitoring of the supervisor as an employee, and Ontario's electronic monitoring policy obligation covers them exactly as it covers workers. The repair is one addition to the onboarding policy deliverable the SIGMA blueprint already promises: name supervisor-cohort operational analytics in the monitoring policy language, state the coaching-not-discipline purpose there, and the appealability the detector already provides becomes a stated policy right rather than a design courtesy.

**Third, check-in completion at supervisor grain risks soft coercion.** Participation in check-ins is voluntary by consent architecture, and the case runs fully by proxy for a worker who declines. A completion percentage displayed to the supervisor of the very workers whose participation is voluntary creates quiet pressure that the consent posture promises away. The repair keeps the metric and moves its audience: check-in completion renders at site and tenant aggregate for employer-side roles, remains at span grain for the coordinator and admin (who already hold individual case access and run the program), and wherever any employer-side surface shows it, the standing sentence rides along: participation is voluntary and non-participation is never a performance matter. One serializer rule and one sentence, and the trust doctrine survives its own analytics.
