# CONTINUUM PROMPT 19: Review of the Recovery Intelligence Memo, with Suggestions

**Source:** Craig Wolf's memo, "Continuous Recovery Intelligence Layer," proposing the category framing, the North Star statement, the one-worker Garda demonstration, per-role screen questions, the SIGMA panel concept, and the closing design principle.
**Verdict in one line:** adopt the framing and the demo shape almost whole, because the built system already implements most of it, and repair five specific collisions with the doctrine Craig's own v2 wiring specification locked, so the story survives counsel, Health Canada, and a skeptical Garda privacy officer in the room.
**Numbering:** requested as Prompt 18; shipped as Prompt 19 because 18 is the admin portal.
**Date:** July 19, 2026

## 1. What the memo gets right, and should become canon

The category claim is the strongest strategic writing this project has produced. "Continuum owns the space between events" is true, differentiating, and honest: it does not claim to replace SIGMA, the physician, or the board, it claims the gap where recovery actually happens and where no incumbent lives. The North Star sentence works as written. The complement positioning (SIGMA manages the claim, Continuum manages the recovery) resolves the buy-versus-fight question before Garda's IT department asks it, and it matches the v2 spec's intake posture exactly.

The per-role question is the second gift, and here is the map the memo does not know it has: every question already names a built surface. "What should I do today" is the worker dashboard's duty list and check-in. "What work is safe today" is the employer dashboard's functional view. "Should treatment or restrictions change" is the clinical portal's recovery signal beside the transition buttons. "Who needs my attention today" is, almost verbatim, the HSE portal's case queue, whose on-screen caption already reads sorted by attention needed. "Which claims require intervention" is the board portal's acknowledgement queue and deadline alerts. "Where are my biggest operational risks" is the employer analytics and the admin platform view. The recommendation is to promote each question to its portal's subtitle, one line of copy per surface, and adopt the who-becomes-smarter test as a standing design gate with one amendment that makes it compatible with everything already ratified: every screen must make its role smarter using only what that role may lawfully see. Intelligence never overrides the visibility matrix; the matrix is what makes the intelligence trustworthy.

The one-worker demonstration is the right shape, and it should run on the working system, not slideware. Marcus Bedard is the worker. The Present mode built in 12H steps his arc from injury through sign-off; the bridge makes every step land differently on four other screens at once; the final scene the memo asks for is already executable: Dr. Owusu presses the gold Clearance button on the clinical portal, the sign-off crosses the bridge, the worker app celebrates, the employer and HSE views retire him, and the board portal closes the claim with the full duty package. The closing line, everyone made better decisions because everyone had better information, is correct and should end the demo. The suggestion: end one beat earlier than the memo does on each screen, letting the audience watch the same single check-in arrive as four different projections, because that moment is the entire company in one keystroke.

## 2. Five collisions, each with its repair

**First, the employer status labels.** The memo puts Plateau Detected and Regression Detected, Clinical Review Recommended on the employer dashboard. That collides with the hardest law in the system: the employer sees functional status only, and regression is a clinical inference computed from scores the employer must never see. Showing a supervisor Regression Detected leaks the shape of the worker's clinical trajectory and hands Garda a discrimination exposure. The repair keeps the idea and moves the vocabulary: the four intelligence labels live on the HSE and clinical surfaces, where they are legitimate and already half-built (the regression engine escalation is in the HSE queue today), while the employer surface speaks functionally: duties on track, duty plan under review, awaiting clinical review. The built masking, where an escalation arrives at the employer as Awaiting review with no trigger detail, is not a limitation to apologize for; it is a demo moment to dramatize, and the privacy officer in the room is the person the demo should be winking at.

**Second, the regulatory line on detected and recommended.** An algorithm that announces Regression Detected and recommends clinical review is, phrased that way, an automated treatment-affecting recommendation, which is the device boundary v1 deliberately stays behind: tracking and visualization with independent clinician review, no scoring, no prediction, no alerts that alter treatment. The repair is wording, not capability: the same threshold rules fire, but the product says escalated to the clinician per program rules, a workflow routing statement, and the clinician's independent review is what changes treatment. The demo script should use that phrasing everywhere, because Health Canada reads marketing.

**Third, predicted outcomes for leadership.** Prediction is not in v1 for the same reason the pose module ships dark. The repair: leadership gets descriptive intelligence now, trends, benchmarks against the 63 day baseline, days regained, and the deck says predictive comes later, built behind the same flag discipline as pose. Under-promising here is a selling point to an enterprise buyer, not a weakness.

**Fourth, the daily worker experience.** Two memo ideas deserve immediate adoption into the worker surface. Morning duty acknowledgement, where the worker confirms each day that they understand what they may and may not do, is a genuinely valuable compliance artifact: a daily timestamped record that the worker knew their restrictions, which is defense evidence, instrumentation, and safety culture in one tap, and it extends the built accept-once lifecycle naturally into an ack-per-day event the HSE portal can display. And the expanded check-in dimensions, fatigue and confidence alongside pain, are cheap to add and clinically meaningful, confidence especially, since fear of reinjury is one of the strongest known predictors of delayed return. Two cautions ride along: the check-in must stay under thirty seconds, so these are tap scales, not questionnaires, and the new dimensions obey the same projection law, never crossing the bridge, feeding only clinician and coordinator views.

**Fifth, the SIGMA panel and the words exercises and guidance.** The SIGMA screen with an embedded Continuum Recovery Intelligence panel is exactly the right picture for GardaWorld and should be built as a clearly labeled illustrative mock, because the pilot integration is intake-level, not live bidirectional, and the demo must not imply otherwise in front of the people who own SIGMA. And the worker's recovery plan, exercises, education, daily guidance, is right so long as authorship stays clinical: the clinician prescribes, the app delivers and tracks. Nothing generative speaks to a worker; AI drafting remains coordinator-only under the v2 rule, and the memo's language should be tightened before it becomes marketing.

## 3. What this means for the build queue

Three small builds convert the memo into demonstrable product. First, the demo script runner: a presenter page that drives Present mode through the memo's beats with the narration lines on screen, so the Garda demonstration is literally a rehearsed instrument. Second, the SIGMA-style claims screen with the live-updating Continuum panel, seeded from the same canon, labeled illustrative. Third, the worker check-in v2: fatigue and confidence tap scales plus morning duty acknowledgement, with the HSE portal showing ack timestamps, all inside the existing privacy tests. The per-portal subtitle copy change is an hour of work across six files and worth doing the same day.

## 5. The design principle, amended and adopted

Who becomes smarter because of this screen, and are they seeing only what they are entitled to see. With that amendment, the memo's principle and the system's law are the same sentence, and the company Craig is describing is the one that has already been built.
