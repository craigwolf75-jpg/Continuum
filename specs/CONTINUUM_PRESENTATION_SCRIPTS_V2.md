## How to run the room with the voice

Gary drives the screens. The agent narrates. The handoff is simple: when a new portal or section is on screen, Gary says a cue out loud, for example: "Walk us through this screen." The agent answers with that section's narration below. If the room asks a question, the agent answers from the scripted answers; anything about price, contracts, or legal goes to Gary by name. If the network drops, the chatbox on each portal shows the same narration in bubbles, and the tappable questions still answer. The presentation never depends on the connection.

Timing guide: about three minutes per portal, twenty five minutes for the full walk, leaving the rest of the hour for questions and the pilot plan.

Presentation order: worker app, employer dashboard, HSE portal, clinical dashboard, board portal, SIGMA connection page, SIGMA portal, admin portal.

## Opening the presentation (spoken before any portal)

"Here is the problem in one sentence. When someone gets hurt at work, five or six different people need to work together: the worker, the employer, the safety coordinator, the clinician, the compensation board, and the company's own HR system. Today they work together by phone calls, emails, and paper forms. Things get missed. People wait. Continuum fixes the coordination, not the medicine. It gives each of those people their own window into one recovery, and each window shows only what that person needs. You are going to see all of those windows in the next half hour, with real seeded cases on screen. One promise before we start: everything you hear today is also built into the product as a switch, a gate, or a rule you can point to. We do not ask you to trust us. We show you where the trust lives."

## PORTAL 1: The worker app

### Opening line

"This is what the injured worker sees on their phone. The whole idea fits in one sentence: thirty seconds of honesty a day."

### Section by section

**The daily check-in.** "Every day, the worker answers a few plain questions. How is the pain, from zero to ten. How is movement. How did work feel, if they worked. That is the entire ask. No forms, no medical words, no homework. Thirty seconds, and they are done. Behind those thirty seconds, the safety rules are always awake: if a worker reports pain of eight or more, the case is escalated to the clinician per program rules, immediately, with no judgment call in between."

**The progress chart.** "Look at the colors. There is no red anywhere on this app, and that is on purpose. We call the rule gold, never red. The chart only draws the days the worker has actually lived, and it grows with them. A hard day shows as a lower point, never as a failure or a warning. Recovery is hard enough without an app scolding you. The worker's app is built to encourage, and the serious signals go to the people whose job it is to act on them."

**The history.** "The worker can look back over their own check-ins any time. It is their record of their own recovery, in their own hands."

**Sign-up, in Settings.** "A new worker joins in four small steps. A welcome that explains what this is. A consent step that is a real choice: the checkbox means something, and saying no is respected. Then the basics, and then their first check-in. From the first minute, the same safety rules are live. Nothing about joining is buried in fine print."

**Pilot mode.** "For tomorrow, this app starts clean. A real worker begins with an empty, honest record, not demonstration data. And the demonstration can be brought back any time for training."

**What leaves this phone.** "One more thing, and it matters. What travels from this app to the coordinator is only the practical part: what work is safe, what needs attention. A diagnosis never leaves. A private feeling never leaves. The worker's medical story is not a data feed."

### The one line to emphasize

"Gold, never red."

### Expected questions

Q: "What if a worker doesn't check in?" A: "Nothing bad happens to the worker. A missed check-in is just a quiet flag for the coordinator to reach out like a human being. Participation is voluntary, and it is never a performance matter."

Q: "What if they report high pain?" A: "A pain report of eight or more is escalated to the clinician per program rules, right away. The app never sits on a serious signal."

### Handoff

"Now let us see what the employer sees. Watch how much smaller this next window is. That is the point."

## PORTAL 2: The employer dashboard

### Opening line

"This is the employer's window, and the first thing to notice is what is not here."

### Section by section

**The overview.** "No diagnoses. No pain scores. No medical notes. Not hidden, not summarized: absent. The employer's real question is simple: what work is safe for this person, and from when. That question never needs a diagnosis to answer. So the employer gets a better answer from less information, and the worker's private health details stay private. When someone is away, the screen names it as an absence, and only as an absence."

**The workers table.** "Each row is a person on a recovery plan, shown in practical terms. Return to work status. What duties are safe. What changed recently. A manager can plan a shift from this screen without ever touching medical information, because there is no medical information here to touch."

**The claims drawer.** "Click a claim and you see the paperwork state in plain words. What has been filed, what is waiting, what needs the employer's attention. Paperwork status, not health status."

**Sign-up, in Settings.** "When an employer joins, before they see anything, they acknowledge two commitments, one at a time, each with a timestamp. First: this is a functional-only view, and they will never see medical details. Second: a worker's participation is voluntary and never a performance matter. Those are not terms of service nobody reads. They are the deal, accepted on the record."

**The bottom navigation.** "Everything the employer can do lives in a handful of tabs. Small on purpose. This seat has the narrowest job, so it gets a narrow, honest tool."

### The one line to emphasize

"A better answer from less information."

### Expected questions

Q: "Can a supervisor see why someone is off?" A: "No. They see that there is an absence and what duties are safe on return. The why is medical, and medical stays with the clinician."

Q: "Can we use check-in data in performance reviews?" A: "No, and you agree to exactly that when you join, on the record, with a timestamp. Participation is voluntary and never a performance matter."

### Handoff

"Next is the seat that holds the whole recovery together: the coordinator."

## PORTAL 3: The HSE portal (the coordinator's seat)

### Opening line

"This is the coordinator's control room. If the worker app is thirty seconds a day, this is where those thirty seconds become action."

### Section by section

**The Return to Work Coordination hub.** "Return to Work, or RTW for short. Seven open cases are on the board right now, with a seventy eight percent check-in completion rate across them. The coordinator sees every active recovery, its stage, and what needs doing next, in one place instead of in seven email threads."

**The case queue.** "Each case shows where the person is in their program and what the next step is. One case here is escalated: Tyler Cardinal, a lower back case at day eighteen. It was escalated to the clinician per program rules. The coordinator did not have to notice it, weigh it, or remember it. The rule did the noticing. The clinician does the deciding."

**The proxy check-in.** "Sometimes a worker cannot check in themselves: no phone that day, a bad morning, a language barrier. The coordinator can enter the check-in for them, and the screen marks it honestly: saved on the record as a coordinator entry. Here is the part that matters: the same escalation rules apply. If the coordinator enters pain of eight or more, it escalates to the clinician exactly as if the worker had typed it. There is no side door around the safety rules."

**The hazard gate.** "This button refuses. Before a worker returns to a duty, someone has to confirm the hazard check was actually done, by a person, at the site. Until then, the confirm button will not go through. Software does not skip safety, and this is what that promise looks like as a button."

**Analytics.** "The measure we watch is written in plain words on the screen: days from restrictions received to first safe onsite duties. In other words, how fast do we get a person back to safe work once we know what is safe. Not back to work at any cost. Back to safe work, measured honestly."

**The Ontario filing.** "The paperwork that goes to the compensation board starts here. In the pilot, it runs on the human path: the coordinator prepares it, a person reviews it, a person sends it. Later, for Ontario, the same paperwork can travel machine to machine, and even then, a person authorizes every send."

**Text messages and quiet hours.** "The system can send text message reminders, and there is a hard rule: a text message never carries case content. Sign-in codes and neutral reminders only, inside quiet hours set to the worker's own time zone. Nobody's medical situation shows up on a lock screen."

**The pilot pack.** "For tomorrow: this portal starts blank, ready for the first real case. There is a New Case form the coordinator fills in a few minutes, and the proxy check-in you just saw works from day one. The demonstration you are watching can be restored for training any time."

### The one line to emphasize

"The button that refuses."

### Expected questions

Q: "What if the coordinator makes the wrong call on something serious?" A: "The serious calls are not the coordinator's to make. The escalation rules act on their own, and everything medical is escalated to the clinician per program rules. The coordinator coordinates. The clinician decides."

Q: "How much training does the coordinator need?" A: "The pilot starts with one coordinator, a blank board, and a one-page runbook. The screens use plain words, every step has one obvious next action, and the first real case is designed to be entered in minutes."

### Handoff

"Everything medical you have heard about ends up in one place. Let us go there."

## PORTAL 4: The clinical dashboard

### Opening line

"This is where every medical decision actually lives. Not shared, not split, not automated. Here."

### Section by section

**The caseload.** "Twenty four active cases. The clinician sees each person's program, their recent check-ins, and anything the rules have escalated."

**The case walkthrough.** "Take Marcus Bedard. He is at day nine of a twenty one day program. His pain today is four, his mobility is six. The clinician sees his real trend in his real numbers, and every note here can be as precise as medicine needs, because this is the one surface built for clinical language. Everywhere else in the platform, we simplify. Here, precision is the point, and simplifying it would be a medical decision, not a wording choice."

**Escalations.** "Whatever the rules escalate arrives here. The pain-eight rule, the flags, the coordinator's concerns. They all land on the clinician's desk because that is the only desk qualified to act on them."

**Return to work authorizations.** "Restrictions and clearances are written here and nowhere else. When the employer sees that a duty is safe, it is because a clinician said so on this screen. And a rule sits underneath all of it: restriction wording is never reworded by anyone without clinician review. Not by us, not by an editor, not by an AI."

### The one line to emphasize

"Where every medical decision actually lives."

### Expected questions

Q: "Does the system ever suggest medical decisions?" A: "No. It routes, it reminds, and it escalates to the clinician per program rules. The clinician decides. Always."

Q: "Can the employer or the board see this screen?" A: "No. This surface exists behind the privacy wall. The employer sees safe duties. The board sees filings. The medicine stays here."

"Now the narrowest window of all: the compensation board."

## PORTAL 5: The board portal

### Opening line

"This is the compensation board seat, and it is the narrowest view on purpose."

### Section by section

**The claims queue.** "Nineteen open claims, seven of them waiting to be acknowledged. The board seat sees claims and filings: what was submitted, when, and what state it is in. It does not see check-ins, notes, or anything clinical, because a claims process needs claims information."

**Milestone alerts.** "Plain-words nudges like: return to work milestone approaching. Enough to keep a claim moving, nothing more."

**The channels.** "Here is the honest map of how paperwork travels in Canada. Three boards can accept machine submissions: Ontario, through a business to business XML channel that returns a confirmation number, plus British Columbia and Alberta with their own formats. The other nine boards work portal assist: Continuum prepares the paperwork perfectly, and a person submits it on the board's own website. We show you which is which, because pretending everything is automated would be a lie with a nice interface."

**The honest retrieval answer.** "And one more piece of honesty that surprises people: no compensation board in Canada offers a way for software to pull claim data back out. Nobody's software can do it, including ours. So anything flowing back, like rebates or claim outcomes, is labeled as an estimate on our screens until a person confirms the real number. When a vendor tells you their system syncs both ways with a board, ask them how."

**The SIGMA exchange section.** "This panel shows filings that involved the SIGMA exchange, rolled up by channel. Filings only. The boundary holds here too: the board seat never sees clinical content."

### The one line to emphasize

"The narrowest view on purpose."

### Expected questions

Q: "Can Continuum pull our claim history from the board?" A: "No, and neither can anyone else's software. No Canadian board publishes a way to pull claim data back out. We prepare and submit where boards allow it, we label everything coming back as an estimate, and a person confirms actuals."

Q: "Is the Ontario machine channel live tomorrow?" A: "No. Tomorrow the paperwork runs on the human path. The machine channel is built and staged for a later phase, and even when it is on, a person authorizes every send."

### Handoff

"Now the part built specifically for this room: how Continuum works next to SIGMA."

## PORTAL 6: The SIGMA connection page

### Opening line

"Before anything else, one word said out loud: proposed. What you are about to see is the proposed workflow between Continuum and SIGMA. Nothing you see here is live today, and nothing about it replaces SIGMA."

### Section by section

**The frame.** "SIGMA is your system of record, serving more than thirty five thousand Canadian employees, and it stays that way. Continuum does not copy your data, does not migrate your data, and does not compete with SIGMA. It consumes a small amount of reference data and returns intelligence. That is the entire relationship."

**The three panels.** "On one side, SIGMA. On the other, Continuum. Between them, a narrow, deliberate bridge. The bridge is small on purpose: the less that crosses, the less there is to secure, audit, and explain."

**The guided walk, seven screens, one case.** "We follow one demonstration case end to end: Jordan Miller, case number GW-2026-1048. Screen by screen: the injury is reported in SIGMA, the way it is today, nothing changes for your intake. The basic reference data crosses the bridge. The recovery is coordinated in Continuum, with everything you saw in the earlier portals. Then the part that saves real time: duty matching. Continuum draws on an occupational database built for this: forty five positions, fifty tasks, twenty seven demand factors, fourteen injury profiles, and two hundred nine pre-matched duties across twenty six categories, organized under a twenty two code restriction system. When the clinician sets restrictions, the system proposes safe duties from that library instead of someone guessing from a binder. And at the end, the paperwork is prepared and held: nothing goes back toward SIGMA or out to a board until a person authorizes the send."

**The privacy panel.** "What crosses the bridge is functional and administrative: safe duties, dates, claim status. Clinical content does not enter SIGMA. Your HR system stays an HR system."

### The one line to emphasize

"Nothing replaced."

### Expected questions

Q: "Is this connected to our SIGMA right now?" A: "Not yet, and we say that plainly. This is the proposed workflow. Building it for real is a scoped project we would do together, with your team controlling what crosses."

Q: "Why should we believe the duty matches?" A: "Because they are proposals, not orders. Every match traces back to the restriction the clinician set and the duty library entry it came from, and a person accepts or rejects each one. The word on the screen is proposed, and it means it."

### Handoff

"Here is the seat your team would use to run that exchange day to day."

## PORTAL 7: The SIGMA portal

### Opening line

"This is the administrator's seat for the exchange. Its rule is five words long: prepared and held, always."

### Section by section

**The overview.** "The state of the exchange at a glance: what is prepared, what has been sent, what needs a person."

**The case exchange.** "These are queues of prepared records. Prepared means Continuum did the work: the record is complete, checked, and ready. Held means it goes nowhere until a person authorizes the send. Every send has a name attached. And notice the empty queue: it says, honestly, that there is nothing waiting. An empty result never dresses itself up to look like a broken connection, and a broken connection never hides behind an empty screen."

**Field mapping.** "Exactly which SIGMA fields map to which Continuum fields, visible on one screen. No black box. Your team can read the entire bridge."

**Sync history.** "Every exchange that has ever happened: what, when, and who authorized it. An audit trail by default, not by request."

**Permissions.** "The privacy wall, written down as settings: what each role may see, and what no role may see. The walls you have heard all morning are rows on this screen."

**Settings.** "One small thing that shows the philosophy: the display name. Your organization calls the system by your own name for it, and choosing it here flips every screen consistently. The platform adapts to your language, not the other way around."

### The one line to emphasize

"Prepared and held, a person authorizes every send."

### Expected questions

Q: "Can this send anything automatically?" A: "No. Every outbound record is prepared and held, and a person authorizes every send, with their name on the history. That is not a setting you have to configure. It is how the seat works."

Q: "What happens if the exchange breaks?" A: "It tells you, plainly. A failure looks like a failure, an empty queue looks like an empty queue, and the sync history shows the last good exchange. Honest states are a feature."

### Handoff

"Last stop: where every promise you heard today lives as a switch."

## PORTAL 8: The admin portal

### Opening line

"This is the administration seat. Everything promised today exists here as a switch, a gate, or an audit line. This is where trust stops being a speech and becomes a screen."

### Section by section

**Tenants.** "The platform runs multiple organizations side by side, each walled from the others: Worley with seven seats, GardaWorld with twelve, Ledcor with five, plus a sample employer for training."

**Guarded capabilities.** "Three advanced capabilities are built into the platform and switched off. Each sits behind its own independent gates, and nothing turns on quietly. You can see the off state with your own eyes right now."

**The CRTW card.** "One of those capabilities deserves its full name: CRTW stands for Corporate Recovery, Training and Workforce Intelligence. It is the analytics layer, and here is its honest status, read straight from the card: it is built into the database now but switched off, and four independent switches must all be on before any score appears anywhere. That staging is deliberate. Predictive scoring ships when the regulatory and consent groundwork says it should, not before, and not quietly."

**The board integrations line.** "The same honesty you saw at the board portal is printed here for administrators: no board offers a way for software to pull claim data back out. We configured our honesty into the product."

**The text message rule.** "The text message content rule, as a setting: sign-in codes and neutral reminders only, never case content, quiet hours stated as clock times in the worker's own time zone."

**Audit lines.** "And underneath it all, the audit trail. The commitments the employer acknowledged, the sends a person authorized, the gates that stayed shut. When someone asks later how we know the rules held, the answer is a screen, not a memory."

### The one line to emphasize

"Every promise as a switch, a gate, or an audit line."

### Expected questions

Q: "Who controls those switches?" A: "Administrators, with independent gates, and every change lands in the audit trail. Nothing turns on quietly, and nothing turns on with one click."

Q: "Does the system score or predict worker outcomes today?" A: "Predictive capability is built into the platform and switched off. Four independent gates must all be opened before any score appears anywhere, and they open when the regulatory and consent groundwork is done, not before."
